    Laminated Shims

    Laminated Shim Applications, Materials, and Thickness Explained

    Laminated shims are precision spacers made by joining multiple thin metal layers to form an adjustable thickness spacer, allowing compensation of dimensional tolerances during assembly. Laminated shims are used in aerospace, automotive, and precision machinery assemblies to ensure component alignment.

    How Laminated Shims Work

    Laminated shims consist of stacked metal layers bonded together across the entire surface with adhesive. Each layer has a defined thickness, typically between 0.025 mm and 0.05 mm depending on the shim configuration. During assembly, individual layers can be removed to adjust the overall spacer thickness.

    Example structure of a laminated shim

    Component > Shim layer (0.05 mm) > Shim layer (0.05 mm) > Shim layer (0.025 mm) > Component

    This layered construction allows compensation of tolerances in mechanical assemblies. In mechanical design, the required spacer thickness is typically determined through tolerance stack-up analysis of the assembled components. Laminated shims allow the calculated clearance to be adjusted during installation without manufacturing a custom spacer or machining solid shims. This simplifies assembly where component alignment is made directly on-site.

    Materials Used in Laminated Shims

    The material is selected according to the mechanical and environmental requirements of the assembly. Common metal materials include:

    Stainless steel – corrosion resistance and high mechanical strength for structural applications

    Aluminum – low weight and good machinability

    Brass – electrical conductivity and non-magnetic properties

    In some configurations, polymer layers are used instead of metal:

    PET (polyester) – electrical insulation and low friction

    POM (polyoxymethylene) – stability and wear resistance.

    Material selection depends on operating conditions such as:

    mechanical load

    temperature range

    corrosion exposure

    required stability.

    Typical Thickness and Layer Structure

    Each laminated layer has a defined thickness. Typical foil thickness ranges between 0.025 mm and 0.05 mm, depending on the shim design and material.

    Layer Thickness Typical Use 0.025 mm Fine adjustment 0.05 mm Standard layer 0.10 mm Coarse adjustment

    The total shim thickness is determined by the number of bonded layers. During assembly, individual layers can be removed to achieve the required spacing. This allows fine adjustment of component position without machining or replacing the shim.

    Typical laminated shim configurations include: multiple metal layers of equal thickness stacked layers combined with a thinner final layer for fine adjustment layer combinations designed for defined tolerance ranges

    A stack of 20 layers of 0.05 mm combined with an additional fine adjustment layer is a typical configuration.

    This layered structure enables precise compensation of dimensional variation in mechanical assemblies.

    Common Applications of Laminated Shims

    Laminated shims are used in mechanical assemblies where precise spacing and alignment are required. They compensate for spacing variations between components and allow accurate adjustment during installation. Typical applications include:

    Aerospace structures – alignment of structural components and control surfaces

    Automotive assemblies – adjustment of gear housings, engines, and drivetrain components

    Industrial machinery – positioning of bearings, shafts, and gearbox housings

    Turbines and heavy equipment – alignment of large mechanical assemblies.

    In these applications, laminated shims allow adjustment of clearances and component position without machining new spacers or modifying existing parts.

    Laminated Shims vs Solid Shims

    Laminated shims and solid shims are both used to create defined spacing between mechanical components. The difference lies in how the required thickness is achieved. Solid shims are manufactured with a fixed thickness. The correct spacer must therefore be selected or machined before assembly.

    Laminated shims consist of multiple thin layers bonded together. Individual layers can be removed during installation to adjust the total thickness. This allows fine alignment directly during assembly.

    Solid shims: FIXED thickness, No thickness adjustment, thickness must be predefined, may require custom machining

    Laminated shims: Adjustable thickness, removable layers, fine alignment during assembly, largely eliminates need for custom machining

    Solid shims are often used where a fixed spacer thickness is sufficient. Laminated shims are preferred where mechanical tolerances must be compensated during installation.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What is the purpose of laminated shims?

    Laminated shims are used to compensate for dimensional tolerances in mechanical assemblies. By removing individual layers, the spacer thickness can be adjusted during installation. This allows precise alignment of components such as bearings, housings, and structural interfaces What thickness are laminated shim layers?

    Individual layers typically range between 0.025 mm and 0.05 mm, depending on the shim design and material. Multiple layers are bonded together to form the required overall thickness. Layers can be removed during assembly to achieve precise spacing.

    What materials are used in laminated shims?

    Common materials include stainless steel, aluminum, and brass. In some applications, polymer foils such as PET or POM are used. Material selection depends on operating conditions such as load, temperature, corrosion exposure, and required dimensional stability.

    Can laminated shims be reused?

    No. Once layers are removed, the remaining shim functions as a fixed-thickness spacer and remains installed for the service life of the assembly.
